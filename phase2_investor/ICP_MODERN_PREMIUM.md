## What they share

Both paths buy because:
1. Existing market is plastic, garish, and shouts (Mr. Clean, Tide, Lysol)
2. Premium tier is fragmented (Method = pretty plastic, Mrs. Meyer's = grandma chic, Blueland = eco-only). Method/Mrs. Meyer's both acquired by SC Johnson, Blueland reportedly ~$170M revenue (journalist-reported). Source: [research/teardowns_and_exits_v2.md](research/teardowns_and_exits_v2.md).
3. No one has built **luxury cleaning as ritual** the way Aesop did for hand soap or Le Labo for fragrance
4. Both will pay $30-80 for a product they currently pay $4-12 for, if the object justifies it `[unverified — directional pricing thesis, not from a willingness-to-pay study]`

## What they don't share (positioning split)

| Lens | Millennial | Gen Z |
|------|-----------|-------|
| Trigger | Aspirational adulthood | Aesthetic autonomy |
| Reference | Architectural Digest editorial | TikTok creator unboxing |
| Price justification | "Worth it. I earned this." | "Worth posting. Tells my story." |
| Distribution priority | Editorial press, hotel partnerships, Goop | Creator seeding, TikTok shop, IG launch |
| Hero ad format | 16:9 cinematic | 9:16 ASMR ritual |

## Conversion hypothesis

- Millennial path closes via **editorial + retail** (Goop, MoMA Store, AD feature).
- Gen Z path closes via **creator + ritual content** (TikTok ASMR, IG Reels, gifted seeding).
- Both paths feed the same waitlist, same brand, same product.

## What this unlocks for investors

Two go-to-market lanes operating in parallel:
- Millennial lane buys the brand (high LTV, retail-led, slower).
- Gen Z lane builds the brand (organic content engine, viral, faster, lower margin per sale).

Together: organic top-of-funnel + premium retail conversion. Cheaper CAC than either alone.
